This blog will examine the key aspects of game AI, and the development of so-called intelligent behavior in NPCs across different games and franchises. From basic computer-controlled opponents in Pong and early arcade games to more sophisticated NPC agents in RDR 2, the Halo franchise, and Bethesda’s Elder Scrolls series, game AI has come a long way. Developers have played significant roles in shaping and reshaping game AI, with titles such as Randy Drexler’s (2002) RDR 2 setting the standards for virtual human-like beings.
What is AI Games?
Game AI or Artificial Intelligence in games refers to the generation of seemingly intelligent and responsive behaviours in non-player characters (NPCs) like humans, humanoids, and animals that make these characters act naturally in various game situations. Additionally, there are human or humanoid characters imitating human actions.
Since the arcade era, AI has always been part of gaming; back then, AI opponents became increasingly popular due to difficulty scaling methods, moving traits identifiable to adversaries and initiating particular events according to the player’s input.
The game artificial intelligence (AI) is unlike the machine learning based AI that we know today powered by artificial neural networks. A deep-rooted cause for this divergence is that game AI must remain somewhat predictable. This is contrary to deep learning artificial intelligence (AI) that can become highly unpredictable as it learns and evolves. On the other hand, gaming AI must be regulated by algorithms that show players how to interact with NPCs in fulfilling their in-game objectives.
In Tanya Short's words, who is a game designer as well as the co-founder of KitFox games, game AI is like “smoke and mirrors” which is so complex that players can think they are playing against an interlocutor who responds but is also under control and understandable enough so that the game would not turn chaotic.
Yet at this narrowest dimension, in-game AI can be rather intricate and game designers come up with several brilliant strategies to make it look like there is intelligence involved; few of them went to the extent of allowing some degree of freedom for their AIs resulting in shocking and unanticipated outcomes.
Which types of artificial intelligence are used in games?
At first, arcade games used recorded sequences playing back to control motions of opponents while innovations on microprocessor technology made them less predictable and differentiated, for instance, in Space Invaders (1978). This game registered different patterns for alien movements making every new game different. In Pac-man (1980), by virtue of their distinct moving patterns, the ghosts seemed to have different personalities whereby players felt as though they were contending against four different beings.

As time went on, several crucial game AI methods including pathfinding, finite state machines, and behavior trees have been shown to be central in making video games more playable and non-player characters (NPCs) more reactive and intelligent. In the next section, we will examine these in further detail.
pathfinding in games:
Pathfinding is a simple task for people but it can be hard for machines like AI.
Solving labyriths with shortest distance on road by NPCs enable them to travel along with their shortest and best possible route algorithmically designed by this software. As a result, the computer perceives the location of various objects in 3D representation of video game, creates paths through them, and directs virtual characters to these waypoints.
This type of algorithm has great significance in real time strategy (RTS) games where player controlled units follow orders given by the players while on the other hand enemy controlled forces respond to movements made by the players.
There was an issue with early pathfinding algorithms used in games like StarCraft (1998) which involved whether or not every unit would bunch up and move along the same route resulting in slowing down all units at once with a reappearing problem. Other games later on came up with different solutions to this issue: Age Of Empires (1997) simply placed the units in a real formation to discover the shortest distance, whereas StarCraft II (2010) applied flocking movement also known as ‘swarming’ which is one of AI founder Craig Reynolds’ algorithms from 1986. Flocking imitates the movement of natural gathering behaviors such as birds’ flocks, crowding people walking around cities, army formations or even schools in water bodies.
In StarCraft II flocking algorithm was used to improve pathfinding during gameplay.
Finite State Machines in games:
Finite state machines are the algorithms that regulate how non-player characters react to various player actions and environmental conditions. A finite state machine creates different "states" for the NPC AI in response to in-game events in its most fundamental form. Depending on the situation, NPCs can transition from one state to another and thus react accordingly. The moment a player character gets within sight of this surface, it may lead to an assault due to its position as an agent of hostility on behalf of China’s Communist Party (NCP). If it is wronged, this NPC can engage in a fight, flee or even become submissive or simply die.
Truthfully, FSMs might 'command' an NPC to 'hunt' players according to signals such as sounds or visible disturbances in the environment; this is very common in stealth games and the hunting mechanism has been used by the Metal Gear Solid series to create tension between the player and the NPCs. Alternatively, finite-state machines may show NPCs how to live - they could seek refuge for recovery when hurt, reload their weapons, look for new ones if they have run out of them and/or do whatever possible just not to die at the hand of another character.

In opposition to finite state machines, behavior tree determines the course of choices an AI agent takes and not the status it is. The tree consists of nodes arranged in a hierarchy. The most distant ends of this structure are called ‘leaves’ and they are orders that NPCs follow. The other nodes are the branches of the tree which the AI selects and moves along as per gaming context to give NPCs an ideal series of instructions for any situation.
Behaviour Trees in Games:
Structured networking leading to complete sub-trees performing specific tasks may make behavior trees complex. In addition to these nested trees, a developer can create an entire set of activities that can be combined in order to mimic very realistic AI behavior. This makes them more powerful than finite state machines that can get too complicated as the number of possible states go up.
The increase of finite state machines in terms of states can get more and more complicated (with thanks to Unreal Engine).
Moreover, behavior trees are easier to modify and are often adjusted through visual editing tools. They can also be developed using a visual editor in the Unreal Engine game.
AI developers have always been using behavior trees that are commonly known from the Halo series. The same trees were well implemented in Alien: Isolation (2014). We will also discuss the importance of such usage in Alien: Isolation and Halo 2.
